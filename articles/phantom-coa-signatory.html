The Phantom "Principal Chemist"

The tell: a COA is signed by an official-sounding "Principal Chemist" — the same name appears on hundreds of documents across multiple vendors, yet that person matches no verifiable professional record anywhere. A real signatory has a footprint. A boilerplate signature line is just decoration.

Why a signature line means so little

An impressive name and title at the bottom of a lab report feels like accountability. But a name is the easiest thing in the world to type. When the same "chemist" signs documents for several unrelated vendors, and you can't find that person in any professional registry, association, or institution, the signature is performing trust rather than providing it.

How to check (2 minutes, free)

  1. Search the exact name + "chemist" and the lab name. A real professional usually has some footprint — a profile, a publication, an institutional page.
  2. Look for the name across other vendors' COAs. If the same signatory appears on documents for unrelated companies, it's likely a template, not a person accountable for your test.
  3. Cross-check the lab itself. A phantom signatory usually sits on top of a captive or fake lab — check the lab's independence and history too.

What good looks like

A genuinely independent lab is an institution with staff, accreditation, and a public track record — its credibility doesn't rest on a single mysterious signature. The signature is the weakest possible trust signal; the lab's independence and a result that verifies on its own portal are what actually count.
